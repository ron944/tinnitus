How This App Works
Tinnitus Finder helps you identify the exact frequency of your tinnitus — the pitch of the ringing, buzzing or hissing sound in your ears. Once identified, that frequency can be used for therapeutic sound treatments.
Step 1 — Find it (Auto Scan)
Press ▶ Start Auto Scan. The app plays tones that automatically sweep through a range of frequencies — from low rumbles to high pitches. It cycles through six different sound types (swept tone, sine wave, pulsed, pink noise, white noise, brown noise) across the full range.

Sit quietly with headphones on and listen for a moment when the sound seems to blend with, echo, or interact with your tinnitus. When that happens, press ☀ That's it! — Pause here. The scan freezes at that frequency.
Step 2 — Pin it (Manual Input)
Once you have a rough frequency, use Manual Input to type it in exactly. Press ♪ Hear Tone to play it. Switch to Find Silence mode (tap the phase card) — at very low volume, the tone will feel like it dissolves into your tinnitus at the exact matching frequency. Fine-tune until you find that point, then press ✓ Save this frequency.
Volume Tips
The app volume slider controls the software level inside the app. Your headset or device volume controls control the hardware output. Both work together — if the app is at 50% and your headset is at 50%, you hear 25% of full output.

Start with the app slider at 30–50% and adjust your headset volume for comfort. For the Find Silence step, lower the app slider to around 10–20%.
Therapy Tab
Once you know your frequency, the Therapy tab lets you play various therapeutic sounds for longer sessions — white noise, pink noise, notched noise (which removes your tinnitus frequency to help the brain recalibrate), binaural beats, and more.
Glossary — What Everything Means
Frequency (Hz)
Frequency is the pitch of a sound, measured in Hertz (Hz). Low numbers (100–500 Hz) are deep, bass sounds like a rumble. High numbers (6,000–14,000 Hz) are sharp, high-pitched sounds like a whistle or hiss. Most tinnitus falls between 4,000 and 10,000 Hz.
Tinnitus
Tinnitus is the perception of sound — ringing, buzzing, hissing, clicking or whooshing — without any external source. It originates in the auditory nervous system, not the ear itself. It affects roughly 1 in 7 adults.
Sine Wave
A pure, single-frequency tone — the simplest possible sound. Like a tuning fork. Best for precisely identifying your tinnitus frequency because there are no other sounds to confuse it.
Swept Tone
A sine wave that slowly wobbles up and down around a centre frequency. Good for the initial scan because it covers a small range around each step, making it easier to notice when you're near a match.
Pulsed Tone (AM)
A sine wave that pulses on and off rhythmically (amplitude modulation). The pulsing makes the tone easier to distinguish from your tinnitus — some people find this easier to match than a continuous tone.
White Noise
A hissing sound that contains all frequencies equally — like a TV with no signal. Good for masking tinnitus broadly. In the scan, it's filtered to focus around each frequency step.
Pink Noise
Like white noise but with more bass — sounds like rain or a waterfall. Many people find it more natural and relaxing than white noise. Widely used in sleep therapy.
Brown Noise
Even deeper and bassier than pink noise — like a strong wind or rumbling river. Preferred by many tinnitus sufferers for long listening sessions or sleep.
Notched Noise
Pink noise with a narrow gap (notch) cut out at your tinnitus frequency. Based on Notched Sound Therapy research — the theory is that removing that frequency from the sound you hear may help the brain gradually reduce its focus on it. Requires knowing your tinnitus frequency first.
Binaural Beat
When two slightly different frequencies are played — one in each ear — your brain perceives a third "beat" at the difference between them. For example, 6,000 Hz in the left ear and 6,010 Hz in the right creates a perceived 10 Hz beat. This can promote relaxation (delta/theta waves) or focus (alpha/beta). Headphones are required.
Find Silence Mode
Plays the tone at a very low volume (8% of your set level). At the exact frequency of your tinnitus, the external tone will seem to "dissolve into" or become indistinguishable from your tinnitus. This is more precise than matching at full volume.
Dwell Time
How long the auto scan stays on each frequency before moving to the next. 3 seconds is a good starting point — long enough to notice an interaction with your tinnitus, short enough that the scan doesn't take forever. Use 5–8 seconds if you need more time to evaluate each frequency.
The 4 kHz Notch
The most common tinnitus frequency. Noise-induced hearing loss (from loud music, machinery, headphone use) typically first damages hearing around 4,000 Hz — and the brain responds by generating a tone to "fill in" the missing sound. This is why 4 kHz tinnitus is so common.
Important Note
This app is a tool for identifying and exploring tinnitus frequencies. It is not a medical device and does not provide diagnosis or treatment. If your tinnitus is new, worsening, or accompanied by hearing loss or dizziness, please consult an audiologist or ENT specialist.
